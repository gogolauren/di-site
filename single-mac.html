A TOUCH OF BRILLIANCE?

Team Work, in "Advanced Usability Test" class

In this project, we tested the touch bar functionality of Apple's New Macbook Pro, and we proposed some recommendations based on our test results.





participants

Our participants ranged from 22-32, graduate and PHD Students, who have been using Apple products for about 10 years. They have been using a Macbook Pro specifically for around 4 years. Everyone is indicated very frequent use of their macbook pro. We recruited our participants from friend groups.

We also conduct a post-questionnaire to get ideas of users' opinions after using the touch bar. Two Likert scale questions are asked. One is "How would you rate your experience with using the touch bar?". Participants' answers are pretty neutral and balanced. Another question is "How likely are you to upgrade your current laptop to this new MacBook Pro after this experience? (only consider the touch bar function)". We only got one positive answer out of 6 responses.











Insights


1. Manu hierarchy
Users always get confused about which menu and how deep they are in a menu. If they open one application, but their mouse is not in the same app, they will not be able to see the touch bar changes with the app.

2. No enough context
When users tapped some buttons on the touch bar, they saw no response or feedback neither on the mouse nor on the screen.

3. Difficulty to view the options
When users tapping the touch bar, their finger will block their view. For example, when users fast forwarded a video on YouTube, they found hard to see the time label in the touch bar.

4. High give up rate
Although we printed these words on the top of tasks for users "Each of the following tasks can be completed by using the touch bar. When testing please try and utilize the touch bar as often as possible. If you feel that you cannot continue using the touch bar to complete the task please do not hesitate to switch and use the touchpad.", we found users often did not consider using touch bar as their first choice. Using task 2 as an example, they used touchpad or keyboard shortcut without even a attempt on the touch bar.

5. Task confusion
We revised our tasks for two times and we conducted two rounds of pilot tests. We tried to be either leading or non-leading in tasks description once, and we found non-leading is better for carrying out the test. After these revision, some users still got confused about what the task wanted them to do. For instance, in task 6 two users ended with adding a bookmark of a web page, which disobeyed the intention of the task.



Recommendations for the touch bar


1. Make buttons on the touchbar functional (ex - fast forward, pause, and play buttons).

2. Add shortcuts for the touchbar (similar to keyboard shortcuts).

3. Make font size of touchbar larger so it is easier to read.

4. Make buttons for common tasks more noticeable (ex - crop button when editing photos).

5. Add physical or audible feedback when editing the touchbar.

6. Add labels to sub-menus so that users know what menu they are in.
